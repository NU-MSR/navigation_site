The Odometry Model

The state of the robot at time \(t\) is

\[\begin{equation} q_t = \begin{bmatrix} \theta_t \\ x_t \\ y_t \end{bmatrix}. \end{equation} \]

The state of the map is \(\begin{bmatrix}m_{x1} & m_{y1} & \dots & m_{xn} & m_{yn} \end{bmatrix}^T \in \mathbb{R}^{2n}\)

Let \(\xi_t = \begin{bmatrix}q_t\\m_t \end{bmatrix}\) be the combined state vector.

The odometry model that we derived earlier in the class governs how the robot's state transitions from time \(t-1\) to time \(t\), given the twist

\[\begin{equation} u_t = \begin{bmatrix} \Delta\theta_t \\ \Delta x_t \\ 0 \end{bmatrix} \end{equation} \]

The \(\Delta y_t\) component of the twist is \(0\) for the diff-drive robot.

The twist \(u\) can be computed from encoder readings or be given as a control input. The forward kinematics of the robot convert encoder readings to a twist.

The Measurement Model

Let \(r_j\) be the distance to landmark \(j\) (this is the range measurement). Let \(\phi_j\) be the relative bearing of landmark \(j\) (this is the bearing measurement).

Relative cartesian \(\bar{x},\bar{y}\) measurements can be transformed into range bearing measurements:

\[\begin{align} r_j &= \sqrt{\bar{x}^2 + \bar{y}^2}\\ \phi_j &= \arctan2(\bar{y}, \bar{x}) \end{align} \]

(The laser scanner gives range-bearing measurements but the landmark detection algorithm provides relative \((\bar{x}, \bar{y}\) measurements).

The measurement model relates the system states to the measurements. The measurement for range and bearing to landmark \(j\) is:

\[\begin{equation} z_j(t) = h_j(\xi_t), \end{equation} \]

where

\[\begin{equation} h_j(\xi_t)= \begin{bmatrix}r_j \\ \phi_j\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} \sqrt{(m_{x,j}-x_t)^2 +(m_{y,j}-y_t)^2} \\ \arctan2(m_{y,j}-y, m_{x,j} - x) - \theta_t \end{bmatrix} + v_t \end{equation} \]

and \(v_t \sim N(0, R)\) is the sensor noise.

We can approximate \(h_j(\xi_t)\) using a Taylor expansion about the estimate \(\hat{\xi}_t\):

\[\begin{equation} h_j(\xi_t) \approx h_j(\hat{\xi}_t) + h'_j(\hat{\xi}_t)(\xi_t-\hat{\xi}_t). \end{equation} \]

Let the estimated relative \(x\) and \(y\) distances be given by

\[\begin{align} \delta_x &= (\hat{x}_t - \hat{m}_{x,j}) \\ \delta_y &= (\hat{y}_t - \hat{m}_{y,j}) \end{align} \]

and let \(d = \hat{\delta}_{t,j}^2 + \hat{\eta}_{t,j}^2\) (this is the estimated squared distance between the robot and landmark \(j\) at time \(t\)).

Then the derivative with respect to the state is:

\[\begin{equation} H_j = h_j'(\xi_t) = \begin{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 0 & \frac{-\delta_x}{\sqrt{d}} & \frac{-\delta_y}{\sqrt{d}} \\ -1 & \frac{\delta{y}}{d} & \frac{-\delta_x}{d} \end{bmatrix} & \begin{bmatrix} 0_{1\times 2 (j-1)}\\ 0_{1\times 2 (j-1)} \end{bmatrix} & \begin{bmatrix} G \frac{\delta_x}{\sqrt{d}} & \frac{\delta_y}{\sqrt{d}} \\ -\frac{\delta_y}{d} & \frac{\delta_x}{d} \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 0_{1\times (n - 2j)}\\ 0_{1\times (n - 2j)} \end{bmatrix} \end{bmatrix} \end{equation} \]

Extended Kalman Filter Slam

At each timestep \(t\), Extended Kalman filter SLAM takes odometry \(u_t\) and sensor measurements \(z_i\) and generates an estimate of the full state vector \(\hat{\xi}_t\).

Initialization

We start out with some guess as to the robot state (usually \((0,0,0)\)) and covariance:

\[\begin{equation} \Sigma_0 = \begin{bmatrix} \Sigma_{0,q} & 0_{2n \times 2n} \\ 0_{2n \times 3} & \Sigma_{0,m} \end{bmatrix} \end{equation} \]

Usually, the initial guess covariance \(\Sigma_{q,0}\) is initialized to zero (indicating that we are certain that the robot is at its initial position. \(\Sigma_{0,m}\) is diagonal, with infinity (or very high numbers) on its diagonal, indicating that the robot does not yet know about any landmarks.

Prediction

First, update the estimate using the model:

\[\begin{equation} \hat{\xi}_t^- = g(\hat{\xi}_{t-1}, u_t, 0) \end{equation} \]

Next, we propagate the uncertainty using the linearized state transition model:

\[\begin{equation} \hat{\Sigma}_t^- = G_t \hat{\Sigma}_{t-1}G_t^T + \bar{Q}, \end{equation} \]

Where

\[\begin{equation} \bar{Q} = \begin{bmatrix} Q & 0_{3\times 2n} \\ 0_{2n\times 3} & 0_{2n \times 2n}\\ \end{bmatrix} \end{equation} \]

is the process noise for the robot motion model, expanding to fill the whole state. Notice that moving does not modify the robot's knowledge of the landmark locations.

Update

A separate sensor measurement step is completed for each landmark that has been observed.

There are practical steps that must occur, beyond what happens in an ordinary EKF, prior to incorporating the measurements.

Once the landmarks are initialized, we incorporate each landmark measurement one-by-one (even if we've received multiple landmark measurements in the same time-step). Since measurements improve our estimate, our linearized model depends on the estimate, and our measurement of landmark \(i\) is not affected by the states of the other landmarks, it makes sense to incrementally incorporate each measurement.
